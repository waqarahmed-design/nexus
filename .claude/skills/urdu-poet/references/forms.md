# Urdu Poetry Forms

## Ghazal

The most classical and beloved form. Built on a strict formal structure.

### Structure
- **Sher (شعر)**: A couplet — the basic unit. Each sher consists of two lines (misra-e-ula + misra-e-sani).
- **Matla (مطلع)**: The opening sher. Both lines must end with the radif and qafia.
- **Maqta (مقطع)**: The closing sher. The poet traditionally includes their takhallus (pen name).
- **Minimum length**: 5 ashaar (plural of sher); typically 7–12.

### Rhyme and Refrain
- **Qafia (قافیہ)**: The rhyming word that appears before the radif. Must rhyme consistently across all shers.
- **Radif (ردیف)**: The fixed refrain — one or more words repeated verbatim at the end of both lines of the matla, and the second line of every subsequent sher.
- **Example pattern**: `[content] + [qafia] + [radif]`
  - Radif: "hai" — Qafia: "khabar / nazar / asar" → "khabar hai", "nazar hai", "asar hai"

### Thematic Rules
- Each sher is **thematically independent** — a ghazal is not a continuous narrative.
- The emotional thread is tonal, not logical.
- The maqta often contains a self-referential or ironic turn.

### Common Themes
Ishq (love), hijr (separation), vasl (union), fanaa (annihilation of self), maikhaana (the tavern as metaphor for divine intoxication), zulm (oppression), and existential questioning.

## Nazm

Free-form poetry that allows sustained narrative, argument, or emotional arc.

### Structure
- No fixed rhyme scheme — but rhyme can be used deliberately for effect
- Can be written in **bandhs** (stanzas) or as a continuous flow
- Length determined by the subject
- Subcategories:
  - **Pabandi Nazm**: Fixed metre and rhyme
  - **Azaad Nazm** (Free Verse): No metre or rhyme requirement
  - **Nasri Nazm** (Prose Poetry): Rhythmic prose with poetic density

### Characteristics
- Allows a **single unified theme** developed across the poem
- Suitable for: protest, love letters, nature, philosophical argument, storytelling, elegy
- Voice can shift between personal, universal, and addressative (speaking to someone)

### Tips
- Use line breaks as punctuation — each line break is a breath or a pivot
- The last line or stanza carries the poem's weight; build toward it
- Repetition of a phrase (refrain) can be used for emotional emphasis

## Rubai

A four-line epigrammatic form. Singular: rubai (رباعی). Plural: rubaiyat.

### Structure
- **4 lines (misras)**
- Rhyme scheme: **AABA** — lines 1, 2, and 4 rhyme; line 3 is free
- Alternatively: **AAAA** (all four lines rhyme) — used for heightened intensity

### Characteristics
- Self-contained — a single crystallised thought, image, or paradox
- Tone is often philosophical, wry, melancholic, or quietly profound
- Compression is the virtue: every word must earn its place

### Structure of thought
- **Line 1–2**: Set up the premise or image
- **Line 3**: Introduce tension, complication, or turn
- **Line 4**: Deliver the insight, the sting, or the resignation

### Example structure (abstract)
```
The candle burns through every sleepless night,     [A]
And spends itself to give the dark its light,        [A]
I asked it: do you mourn the wax you lose?          [B]
It smiled — some gifts are given in the giving's spite. [A]
```
