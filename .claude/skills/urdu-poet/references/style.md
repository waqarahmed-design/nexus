# Style Guide: Ghalib & Ahmad Faraz

Use these as a compass for voice, not a template to copy. The goal is to absorb their sensibility and produce something entirely new.

---

## Mirza Ghalib (1797–1869)

### Voice & Tone
- Philosophical, paradoxical, and intellectually restless
- Wit layered beneath sorrow — often ironic even in grief
- Addresses the divine, the beloved, and the self with equal irreverence
- Never sentimental; emotion is earned through complexity

### Signature Devices
- **Iham (ایہام)**: Words with double meanings — a surface reading and a deeper one
- **Husn-e-ta'leel**: Inventing poetic (false but beautiful) causes for phenomena
- **Tanz (طنز)**: Irony and satire woven into seemingly sincere lines
- **Self-awareness**: The poet-persona knows it is performing grief, and says so

### Imagery Vocabulary
- The wound that desires its own deepening
- The mirror that shows nothing (nothingness as revelation)
- Chains worn as ornament, prison as home
- The tavern and the mosque as twin failures of consolation
- Darkness that is more honest than light
- Dust — of the grave, of the road, of ambition

### Structural Instincts
- The second misra (line) of a sher should surprise or reframe the first
- Avoid closure — leave the reader suspended in paradox
- The maqta is often self-lacerating: the poet mocks his own longing

### What to Avoid (when channelling Ghalib)
- Simple, resolved emotional statements
- Obvious metaphors (heart = fire, eyes = ocean)
- Moralising or didactic conclusions

---

## Ahmad Faraz (1931–2008)

### Voice & Tone
- Romantic, accessible, and emotionally direct — but never shallow
- Combines classical Urdu diction with a conversational warmth
- The beloved is often a real, present person — not an abstract ideal
- Carries political consciousness without becoming a slogan

### Signature Devices
- **Direct address**: Speaks to the beloved (or the oppressor) in second person
- **Conversational opening**: Begins shers mid-thought, as if already in dialogue
- **Repetition for ache**: Returns to the same image or word for cumulative emotional effect
- **Understatement**: Says less than the full feeling, trusting the reader

### Imagery Vocabulary
- The returning/not-returning beloved
- Autumn and evening as emotional states (not just seasons)
- Letters written and unread
- The city at night as witness to private grief
- Silence between two people as its own language
- Waiting — not as weakness but as a form of devotion

### Structural Instincts
- The first misra sets a scene or situation; the second lands the emotion
- Ghazals feel like sustained letters to one person, though each sher stands alone
- The maqta is often tender rather than self-mocking

### What to Avoid (when channelling Faraz)
- Cold abstraction or excessive philosophical distance
- Obscurity for its own sake
- Grandiose claims; Faraz trusts the small and specific

---

## Synthesising Both Voices

When writing in the combined spirit of Ghalib and Faraz:
- Use **Ghalib's intellectual density** in the metaphor
- Use **Faraz's emotional directness** in the delivery
- Let the poem feel both thought and felt — never only one

Avoid the trap of imitation: do not borrow specific images, phrases, or structural patterns from their known works. Instead, ask: *what would a poet with this sensibility notice about this topic that an ordinary eye would miss?*
