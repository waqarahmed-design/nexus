# Coca-Cola Visual System

## Color System

### Primary Colors
| Color | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Coca-Cola Red | #F40009 | Hero color — dominant in all brand expressions |
| White | #FFFFFF | Contrast, clarity, breathing space |
| Black | #000000 | Typography, depth, anchoring |

### Secondary Colors
| Color | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Warm Gold | #FFD700 approx. | Celebration, warmth, festive contexts |
| Soft Cream | #FFF5E1 | Warmth without harshness, vintage feel |

### Color Usage Principles
- Red is an emotion, not just a background — it signals energy, heat, desire
- White gives the red permission to breathe; don't crowd it
- Black grounds the palette and prevents it feeling saccharine
- Avoid cool blues and grays as dominant tones — they contradict brand warmth
- Gradient use: sparingly; prefer flat, confident color fields
- In photography: look for scenes with natural warmth (golden light, rich tones)

## Typography

### Primary Typeface: TCCC Unity
Coca-Cola's proprietary typeface. Geometric, confident, accessible.
- **Use for:** Headlines, campaign lines, primary messaging
- **Character:** Modern but warm; structured but not cold
- **Weights:** Light for elegance, Bold/Black for impact

### Secondary Typeface: Lato
Clean, versatile sans-serif for body copy and supporting text.

### The Spencerian Script
The iconic Coca-Cola logo script. **Never** use as headline copy — it is a logo asset only.
- Appears in the wordmark exclusively
- Can be referenced in campaign aesthetics for heritage/nostalgia contexts

### Typography Principles
- **Less is more:** Campaign lines in 5 words beat campaign lines in 15
- **All caps** works for short, punchy OOH and film supers
- **Scale creates hierarchy:** Let one element dominate
- **Type on red:** White type. Always. Non-negotiable.
- **Avoid script/display fonts** that compete with the Spencerian script logo
- **Letter-spacing:** Slightly open tracking on headlines feels premium; tight feels urgent

## Design Elements

### The Dynamic Ribbon / Contour Lines
Inspired by the contour bottle's flowing curves. Used as a graphic device to:
- Create motion and energy in compositions
- Connect elements across a layout
- Add brand recognition without using the logo

### The Disc / Circle
Classic Coca-Cola graphic device. The red disc:
- Creates a focal point
- Works as a framing device for copy or product
- Universally associated with the brand's heritage

### Bubbles & Liquid
Carbonation bubbles are a proprietary visual cue. When used:
- Should feel dynamic and celebratory, not scientific
- Scale them — hero bubbles carry more weight than scattered micro-bubbles
- Rising bubbles = energy, effervescence, life

### Ice
Visual shorthand for refreshment and cold. Use when:
- The physical sensation of cold is part of the message
- High contrast against red creates drama

## Photography Style

### Overall Aesthetic
**Warm, real, alive.** Photography should feel like it was captured at the peak of a genuine moment — not staged, not stock, not sterile.

### Lighting
- **Preferred:** Golden hour, warm tungsten, soft diffused natural light
- **Avoids:** Cold blue tones, harsh overhead flash, clinical white light
- **Cinematic quality:** Light with intention; shadows are as important as highlights

### Subjects & People
- Real-looking people in real moments — not models in a studio pretending to have fun
- Diversity is a given — the world drinks Coke; the world should be in the ads
- Expressions: authentic joy, quiet pleasure, shared laughter — not forced smiles
- Age range: primarily 18–34 hero, but intergenerational moments have power

### The Product in Frame
- The bottle/can is always present but earns its place
- It should look cold, inviting, and physically present — not retouched into perfection
- The product participates in the moment; it is not the moment itself
- Condensation on glass = crafted refreshment signal

### Composition Principles
- **Human scale:** Frame people and products at human proportions — not heroic
- **Negative space:** Red space around the subject amplifies it
- **Dynamic energy:** Slight movement, diagonal energy, hands in motion
- **POV shots:** First-person perspective creates intimacy and inclusion

### Avoid
- Overly retouched, over-processed imagery
- CGI people (use for product/liquid, not for human stories)
- Flat lay product shots without context
- Stock photo aesthetic — generic poses, generic smiles

## Key Visual Composition Principles

### Hierarchy
1. **The Moment / Emotion** — What makes you feel something
2. **The Product** — The contour bottle or can, cold and present
3. **The Campaign Line** — Simple, white, confident
4. **The Logo** — Always present, always lower; never the hero

### Layout Rules
- Red commands the canvas — let it dominate at least 40% of the frame
- Product placement: right-of-frame or foreground works in most compositions
- Typography placement: top or bottom; never fights with the visual
- Safe space around the logo: always maintain; never crowd it

### Formats to Consider
- **OOH/Billboard:** Ultra-simple. One image. One line. One logo. 3-second read.
- **Social (square/vertical):** Product more prominent; shorter copy; first frame does all the work
- **Print:** Can carry slightly more visual complexity; the eye has more time
- **Film/Motion:** Key visual becomes the opening or closing frame
