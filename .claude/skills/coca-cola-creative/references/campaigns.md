# Coca-Cola Landmark Campaigns

Use these as a creative benchmark — understand what made them great, then build something new.

---

## "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing" (1971)
**Agency:** McCann Erickson
**Platform:** TV

**The Idea:** Young people from every nation gather on a hillside in Italy, each holding a Coke, singing a song of peace and unity.

**Why It Worked:**
- Released during Vietnam War — optimism felt radical, not naive
- The product was secondary; humanity was primary
- A simple, singable melody made it culturally sticky
- It didn't sell refreshment — it sold a feeling about the world

**Creative Lesson:** When the world is divided, Coca-Cola can be the thing that unites without preaching. The brand takes a position by gathering people, not by having an opinion.

---

## Mean Joe Greene (1979)
**Agency:** McCann Erickson
**Platform:** TV (Super Bowl)

**The Idea:** A tired, injured NFL star (Mean Joe Greene) is offered a Coke by a young boy. He drinks it. He smiles. He throws the boy his jersey.

**Why It Worked:**
- A single, earned human moment in 60 seconds
- The "magic" came from the transformation — the drink changed something
- Nothing was said about the product's taste or quality
- Made a tough man vulnerable; made a child powerful

**Creative Lesson:** The most powerful Coke ads require almost no words. Find the moment. Let it breathe. Let the product be the catalyst.

## "Always Coca-Cola" (1993)
**Platform:** TV / Global Campaign

**The Idea:** A series of upbeat, colorful vignettes — polar bears, beach scenes, everyday moments — all connected by the feeling of Coke and an earworm jingle.

**Why It Worked:**
- The "polar bears" execution became a cultural icon
- Simplicity and joy — no complicated narrative
- The word "always" communicated permanence and reliability
- Works across cultures because joy is universal

**Creative Lesson:** Sometimes a campaign doesn't need a deep human truth. Sometimes pure, unfiltered delight — executed perfectly — is the idea.

## "Share a Coke" (2011)
**Agency:** Ogilvy, Australia (rolled out globally)
**Platform:** Integrated 360°

**The Idea:** Replace the Coca-Cola logo on bottles with the 150 most popular names in each country. "Share a Coke with [Name]."

**Why It Worked:**
- Turned a mass product into a personal one
- Created a scavenger hunt — people hunted for their name
- Generated billions of social media shares organically
- The idea was the product change itself — advertising followed behavior

**Creative Lesson:** The best Coke ideas can live in the product, not just in media. When the idea and the product are the same thing, you don't need a big budget.

## "Open Happiness" (2009–2016)
**Agency:** Wieden+Kennedy
**Platform:** Global integrated

**The Idea:** Happiness is everywhere and in everyone — Coca-Cola opens the door to it.

**Why It Worked:**
- A platform flexible enough to work across 200 markets
- "Open" had double meaning: open a Coke, open yourself to happiness
- Campaign executions ranged from heartfelt to playful without feeling inconsistent
- The simplicity of the line made it globally translatable

**Creative Lesson:** The best campaign platforms are simple enough to interpret endlessly. A great campaign line is a brief, not a headline.

---

## "Taste the Feeling" (2016–2021)
**Agency:** Mercado-McCann / Sra. Rushmore / Santo
**Platform:** Global integrated

**The Idea:** Return to product-centered advertising — the simple pleasure of drinking a Coke.

**Why It Worked:**
- Deliberately pulled back from big social statements
- The product — ice cold, effervescent, satisfying — was the hero
- Universal human moments (first date, after sport, with family) provided emotional context
- Reclaimed the sensory experience at a time when purpose-led ads dominated

**Creative Lesson:** Don't forget the product. Sometimes the most powerful insight is: this drink tastes good, and that's enough.

## "Real Magic" (2021–Present)
**Agency:** WPP Open X / Ogilvy
**Platform:** Global integrated

**The Idea:** Real human connection — even the smallest, most fleeting moment — is the most magical thing in the world. Coca-Cola is present in those moments.

**Key Executions:**
- **"Pixel Point"** — Gamers from rival factions connect over a Coke; the game world becomes real
- **"One Coke Away From Each Other"** — Famous portraits throughout history reimagined as if the subjects shared a Coke
- **"Be Who You Are"** — Celebrating the magic of people being authentically themselves, connected by Coke

**Why It Works:**
- Addresses post-pandemic longing for real connection
- "Magic" is not supernatural — it's the heightened feeling of a genuine human moment
- Works across the digital/physical divide
- Broad enough to encompass any cultural context

**Creative Lesson:** The best platforms are built on a human truth so universal that they can run for years and across every market.

## Patterns in Great Coca-Cola Work

1. **The catalyst, not the hero** — Coke enables the moment; it doesn't create it artificially
2. **Simplicity is the ambition** — Every great idea can be explained in one sentence
3. **Emotion over information** — No great Coke ad has ever led with flavour notes or ingredients
4. **The product must appear cold** — Physical desirability is always present, even in emotional campaigns
5. **Music matters** — More great Coke campaigns have iconic soundtracks than any other brand
6. **The world is the cast** — Diversity isn't a mandate; it's a reflection of who actually drinks Coke
7. **Avoid being a mirror** — Coke doesn't reflect culture cynically; it aspires toward the best version of it
